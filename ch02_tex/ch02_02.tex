\section*{Chapter 2, Problem 2}

\subsection*{Problem Statement}
\textit{The shortest line (three dimensions).} Prove that the shortest distance between two points in three-dimensional space is a straight line. Take the origin and $(a, b, c)$ as the two points and actually determine the equation of the line.

\subsection*{Solution}

In three dimensions, the arc length element is
\[
  d\ell = \sqrt{dx^2 + dy^2 + dz^2}.
\]
Let us parametrize the curve by $x$, writing $y = y(x)$ and $z = z(x)$. Then the total length is
\[
  L = \int_0^a \sqrt{1 + y'^2 + z'^2}\,dx,
\]
where $y' = dy/dx$ and $z' = dz/dx$.

The integrand $f(y, z, y', z') = \sqrt{1 + y'^2 + z'^2}$ does not depend explicitly on $x$, $y$, or $z$. The Euler--Lagrange equations for $y$ and $z$ are:
\[
  \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} - \frac{d}{dx}\frac{\partial f}{\partial y'} = 0
  \quad \text{and} \quad
  \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} - \frac{d}{dx}\frac{\partial f}{\partial z'} = 0.
\]
Since $\partial f/\partial y = 0$ and $\partial f/\partial z = 0$, we get
\[
  \frac{d}{dx}\frac{\partial f}{\partial y'} = 0
  \quad \text{and} \quad
  \frac{d}{dx}\frac{\partial f}{\partial z'} = 0.
\]
Computing the partial derivatives:
\[
  \frac{\partial f}{\partial y'} = \frac{y'}{\sqrt{1 + y'^2 + z'^2}} = A,
  \qquad
  \frac{\partial f}{\partial z'} = \frac{z'}{\sqrt{1 + y'^2 + z'^2}} = B,
\]
where $A$ and $B$ are constants. From the first equation:
\[
  y'^2 = A^2(1 + y'^2 + z'^2).
\]
From the second:
\[
  z'^2 = B^2(1 + y'^2 + z'^2).
\]
Dividing:
\[
  \frac{y'^2}{z'^2} = \frac{A^2}{B^2}
  \quad \Longrightarrow \quad
  y' = \frac{A}{B}\,z' \quad (\text{assuming } B \neq 0).
\]
Since both $A$ and $B$ are constants, we can write $y' = c_1$ and $z' = c_2$ for constants $c_1$ and $c_2$. Integrating:
\[
  y = c_1 x + d_1, \qquad z = c_2 x + d_2.
\]
Applying the boundary conditions $y(0) = 0$, $z(0) = 0$, $y(a) = b$, $z(a) = c$:
\[
  d_1 = 0, \quad d_2 = 0, \quad c_1 = \frac{b}{a}, \quad c_2 = \frac{c}{a}.
\]
Therefore:
\[
  \boxed{y = \frac{b}{a}\,x, \qquad z = \frac{c}{a}\,x,}
\]
which is the parametric equation of the straight line from the origin to $(a, b, c)$, confirming that the shortest path is indeed a straight line.

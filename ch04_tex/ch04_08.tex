\begin{center}
{\Large\bfseries Chapter 4, Problem 8}\\[6pt]
Byron \& Fuller, \textit{Mathematics of Classical and Quantum Physics}
\end{center}

\bigskip

\textbf{Problem.} In classical mechanics, the virial theorem states that the average value of the potential energy of a simple harmonic oscillator is equal to the average value of its kinetic energy. If $\langle\phi, A\phi\rangle$ denotes the average value in the state $\phi$ of an observable $A$ (assuming that $\phi$ is normalized to unity), show that this result is also true for the quantum-mechanical harmonic oscillator. Show that the polarizability of an electron bound by a harmonic force is the same as is found classically, namely $e^2/k$, where $e$ is the electric charge and $k$ is the spring constant. This means that the dielectric constant for a collection of such objects (density $= n$ per unit volume) at zero frequency is $4\pi n e^2/k$.

\bigskip
\textbf{Solution.}

The quantum harmonic oscillator Hamiltonian is:
\[
H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = T + V,
\]
where $T = p^2/(2m)$ is the kinetic energy and $V = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$ is the potential energy, with $k = m\omega^2$.

Using creation and annihilation operators $a^\dagger$ and $a$:
\[
x = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}}(a + a^\dagger), \quad p = i\sqrt{\frac{m\omega\hbar}{2}}(a^\dagger - a),
\]
\[
H = \hbar\omega\left(a^\dagger a + \frac{1}{2}\right).
\]

The energy eigenstates $|n\rangle$ satisfy $H|n\rangle = \hbar\omega(n + \tfrac{1}{2})|n\rangle$.

\textbf{Expectation value of $V$:}
\begin{align*}
\langle n|V|n\rangle &= \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 \langle n|x^2|n\rangle = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 \cdot \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}\langle n|(a+a^\dagger)^2|n\rangle.
\end{align*}
Now $(a+a^\dagger)^2 = a^2 + aa^\dagger + a^\dagger a + (a^\dagger)^2$. The only terms with nonzero diagonal matrix elements are $aa^\dagger = a^\dagger a + 1$ and $a^\dagger a$:
\[
\langle n|(a+a^\dagger)^2|n\rangle = \langle n|a^\dagger a|n\rangle + \langle n|aa^\dagger|n\rangle = n + (n+1) = 2n+1.
\]
Therefore:
\[
\langle V\rangle = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 \cdot \frac{\hbar}{2m\omega}(2n+1) = \frac{\hbar\omega}{4}(2n+1) = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right).
\]

\textbf{Expectation value of $T$:}
\[
\langle T\rangle = \langle H\rangle - \langle V\rangle = \hbar\omega\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) - \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right).
\]

Hence $\langle T\rangle = \langle V\rangle$, confirming the quantum virial theorem for the harmonic oscillator.

\bigskip
\textbf{Polarizability:}

In an external electric field $\mathcal{E}$, the perturbed Hamiltonian is:
\[
H' = H - e\mathcal{E}x = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}kx^2 - e\mathcal{E}x.
\]
Completing the square:
\[
H' = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2}k\left(x - \frac{e\mathcal{E}}{k}\right)^2 - \frac{e^2\mathcal{E}^2}{2k}.
\]

This is just a shifted harmonic oscillator. The ground state energy is:
\[
E_0' = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega - \frac{e^2\mathcal{E}^2}{2k}.
\]

The induced dipole moment is:
\[
\langle d \rangle = -\frac{\partial E_0'}{\partial \mathcal{E}} = \frac{e^2\mathcal{E}}{k} = \alpha\mathcal{E},
\]
giving the polarizability $\alpha = e^2/k$, which is exactly the classical result.

For $n$ oscillators per unit volume, the dielectric constant at zero frequency is:
\[
\epsilon = 1 + 4\pi n\alpha = 1 + \frac{4\pi n e^2}{k}.
\]

(In Gaussian units, the susceptibility contribution is $4\pi n e^2/k$.) \qed

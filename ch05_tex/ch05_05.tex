\section*{Problem 5.5}

\textbf{Problem:} Prove that if $f(x)$ is real, then
\[
\tilde{f}(k) = \tilde{f}^*(-k),
\]
where $\tilde{f}(k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,e^{ikx}\,dx$ is the Fourier transform of $f$.

\bigskip
\textbf{Solution:}

The Fourier transform of $f(x)$ is defined as:
\[
\tilde{f}(k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,e^{ikx}\,dx.
\]

Taking the complex conjugate:
\[
\tilde{f}^*(k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f^*(x)\,e^{-ikx}\,dx.
\]

Since $f(x)$ is real, $f^*(x) = f(x)$, so:
\[
\tilde{f}^*(k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,e^{-ikx}\,dx.
\]

Now evaluate $\tilde{f}^*(-k)$ by replacing $k$ with $-k$:
\[
\tilde{f}^*(-k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,e^{-(-k)ix}\,dx = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,e^{ikx}\,dx = \tilde{f}(k).
\]

Therefore:
\[
\boxed{\tilde{f}(k) = \tilde{f}^*(-k).}
\]

This result means that for real functions, the real part of the Fourier transform is an even function of $k$, and the imaginary part is an odd function of $k$:
\[
\operatorname{Re}\tilde{f}(k) = \operatorname{Re}\tilde{f}(-k), \qquad \operatorname{Im}\tilde{f}(k) = -\operatorname{Im}\tilde{f}(-k).
\]

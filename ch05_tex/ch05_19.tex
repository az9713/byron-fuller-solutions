\section*{Problem 5.19}

\textbf{Problem:} It should be clear from the definitions that pointwise convergence does not imply uniform convergence. The question comes up, does pointwise convergence imply convergence in the mean? Show that the sequence of functions
\[
f_n(x) = 0
\]
converges pointwise for all $x$ to the function zero, that is,
\[
\lim_{n\to\infty} f_n(x) = 0,
\]
but does not converge in the mean to the function zero, i.e.,
\[
\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_0^1 |f_n(x)|^2\,dx \neq 0.
\]

(The actual sequence from the text is something like $f_n(x) = \sqrt{n}$ on $(0,1/n)$ and $0$ elsewhere.)

\bigskip
\textbf{Solution:}

Consider the sequence of functions on $[0,1]$ defined by:
\[
f_n(x) = \begin{cases} \sqrt{n} & \text{if } 0 < x < 1/n, \\ 0 & \text{if } 1/n \leq x \leq 1. \end{cases}
\]

\subsection*{Pointwise convergence to zero}

For any fixed $x \in (0,1]$, choose $N$ such that $1/N < x$. Then for all $n > N$, we have $x > 1/n$, so $f_n(x) = 0$. Therefore:
\[
\lim_{n\to\infty} f_n(x) = 0 \quad \text{for all } x \in (0,1].
\]

At $x = 0$: $f_n(0) = \sqrt{n} \to \infty$, but this is a set of measure zero and doesn't affect the integral. In any case, for every $x > 0$, the pointwise limit is zero.

\subsection*{Does NOT converge in the mean}

The $L^2$ norm is:
\[
\|f_n\|^2 = \int_0^1 |f_n(x)|^2\,dx = \int_0^{1/n} n\,dx = n \cdot \frac{1}{n} = 1.
\]

Therefore:
\[
\lim_{n\to\infty}\int_0^1|f_n(x)|^2\,dx = 1 \neq 0.
\]

\subsection*{Conclusion}

The sequence $\{f_n\}$ converges pointwise to the zero function for all $x > 0$, but:
\[
\|f_n - 0\|^2 = \int_0^1|f_n(x)|^2\,dx = 1 \quad \text{for all } n.
\]

Thus $f_n$ does \textbf{not} converge to zero in the mean (in the $L^2$ sense). This demonstrates that:

\[
\boxed{\text{Pointwise convergence does NOT imply convergence in the mean.}}
\]

The intuition is that although $f_n$ becomes zero at every point, the ``spike'' near $x = 0$ grows taller (height $\sqrt{n}$) even as it grows narrower (width $1/n$), keeping the total area under $|f_n|^2$ constant.

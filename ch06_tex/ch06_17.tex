\textbf{Chapter 6, Problem 17.} Compute, using Cauchy's integral formula:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] $\displaystyle\oint_C \frac{e^{iz}}{z^2}\,dz$, \quad $C$: boundary of a square with sides $x = \pm 2$, $y = \pm 2$.
\item[(b)] $\displaystyle\oint_C \frac{z^2}{z-2}\,dz$, \quad $C$: circle of radius 1, center $i$.
\end{itemize}
Both contours are oriented counterclockwise.

\bigskip
\textbf{Solution.}

\textbf{(a)} The integrand $e^{iz}/z^2$ has a pole of order 2 at $z = 0$. The square with vertices at $(\pm 2, \pm 2)$ encloses $z = 0$.

By Cauchy's integral formula for derivatives: if $f(z)$ is analytic inside and on $C$, then
\[
\oint_C \frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz = \frac{2\pi i}{n!}f^{(n)}(z_0).
\]

Here $f(z) = e^{iz}$, $z_0 = 0$, $n = 1$:
\[
\oint_C \frac{e^{iz}}{z^2}\,dz = \frac{2\pi i}{1!}f'(0) = 2\pi i \cdot (ie^{iz})\big|_{z=0} = 2\pi i \cdot i = -2\pi.
\]

\[
\boxed{\oint_C \frac{e^{iz}}{z^2}\,dz = -2\pi}
\]

\textbf{(b)} The integrand $z^2/(z-2)$ has a simple pole at $z = 2$. The circle of radius 1 centered at $i$ consists of points $|z - i| = 1$. We check: $|2 - i| = \sqrt{4+1} = \sqrt{5} > 1$, so $z = 2$ is \emph{outside} the contour.

Since $z^2/(z-2)$ is analytic inside and on $C$ (no singularities enclosed), by the Cauchy--Goursat theorem:
\[
\boxed{\oint_C \frac{z^2}{z-2}\,dz = 0}
\]

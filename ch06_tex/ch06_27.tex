\textbf{Chapter 6, Problem 27.} Derive a dispersion relation for $f(z)$ when $|f(z)|$ tends to a constant as $|z| \to \infty$.

\bigskip
\textbf{Solution.}

If $f(z)$ is analytic in the upper half-plane but $|f(z)| \to C$ (a constant) as $|z| \to \infty$ rather than $f(z) \to 0$, the standard dispersion relation fails because the semicircular contour integral doesn't vanish.

\textbf{Subtracted dispersion relation.} Define:
\[
g(z) = \frac{f(z) - f(z_0)}{z - z_0},
\]
where $z_0$ is a fixed point (in the upper half-plane or on the real axis). Then $g(z) \to 0$ as $|z| \to \infty$, so the standard dispersion relation applies to $g$.

Applying Cauchy's formula to $g$ and simplifying:
\[
f(z) = f(z_0) + \frac{z - z_0}{\pi i}\mathcal{P}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{f(x)}{(x-z)(x-z_0)}\,dx.
\]

For real $z = \alpha$ and real $z_0 = \alpha_0$:
\[
f(\alpha) = f(\alpha_0) + \frac{\alpha-\alpha_0}{\pi i}\mathcal{P}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{f(x)}{(x-\alpha)(x-\alpha_0)}\,dx.
\]

Separating into real and imaginary parts ($f = u + iv$):
\[
u(\alpha) = u(\alpha_0) + \frac{\alpha-\alpha_0}{\pi}\mathcal{P}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{v(x)}{(x-\alpha)(x-\alpha_0)}\,dx,
\]
\[
v(\alpha) = v(\alpha_0) - \frac{\alpha-\alpha_0}{\pi}\mathcal{P}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{u(x)}{(x-\alpha)(x-\alpha_0)}\,dx.
\]

These are the \textbf{once-subtracted dispersion relations}. They converge even when $f(z) \to C$ at infinity, because the extra factor $(x-\alpha_0)$ in the denominator provides additional convergence.

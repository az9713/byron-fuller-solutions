\textbf{Chapter 6, Problem 28.} Prove that a uniformly convergent series of analytic functions can be integrated term by term. Hence show that a uniformly convergent series of analytic functions is an analytic function. [\textit{Hint:} Use Morera's theorem of Problem 21.]

\bigskip
\textbf{Solution.}

\textbf{Part 1: Term-by-term integration.}

Let $f_n(z)$ be analytic in a region $R$ and suppose $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n(z)$ converges uniformly to $f(z)$ on every compact subset of $R$. Let $C$ be any contour in $R$. We need to show:
\[
\int_C f(z)\,dz = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\int_C f_n(z)\,dz.
\]

Let $S_N(z) = \sum_{n=1}^{N}f_n(z)$. By uniform convergence, for any $\epsilon > 0$ there exists $N_0$ such that for $N > N_0$:
\[
|f(z) - S_N(z)| < \epsilon \quad \text{for all } z \in C.
\]

Then:
\[
\left|\int_C f(z)\,dz - \sum_{n=1}^{N}\int_C f_n(z)\,dz\right| = \left|\int_C [f(z)-S_N(z)]\,dz\right| \leq \epsilon \cdot L(C),
\]
where $L(C)$ is the length of $C$. Since $\epsilon$ is arbitrary, the result follows.

\textbf{Part 2: Analyticity of $f(z)$.}

Each $f_n(z)$ is analytic, so by Cauchy's theorem $\oint_C f_n(z)\,dz = 0$ for every closed contour $C$ in $R$. By Part 1:
\[
\oint_C f(z)\,dz = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\oint_C f_n(z)\,dz = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} 0 = 0.
\]

Also, $f(z)$ is continuous as the uniform limit of continuous functions.

By Morera's theorem (Problem 21): since $f(z)$ is continuous in $R$ and $\oint_C f(z)\,dz = 0$ for every closed contour $C$ in $R$, $f(z)$ is analytic in $R$.

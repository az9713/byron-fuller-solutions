\textbf{Chapter 6, Problem 29.} Find the order of the pole at the origin, the residue there, and the integral around a small path $C$ enclosing the origin, but no other singularities, for each of the following functions:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] $\cot z$
\item[(b)] $\csc^2 z \log(1-z)$
\item[(c)] $\displaystyle\frac{\sin z - \tan z}{z}$
\end{itemize}

\bigskip
\textbf{Solution.}

\textbf{(a) $\cot z = \cos z/\sin z$:}

At $z = 0$: $\sin z = z - z^3/6 + \cdots$ has a simple zero, and $\cos 0 = 1 \neq 0$.

So $\cot z$ has a \textbf{simple pole} (order 1) at $z = 0$.

Residue:
\[
\text{Res}_{z=0}\cot z = \lim_{z\to 0}z\cot z = \lim_{z\to 0}\frac{z\cos z}{\sin z} = 1.
\]

Integral: $\oint_C \cot z\,dz = 2\pi i \cdot 1 = \boxed{2\pi i}$.

\textbf{(b) $\csc^2 z\log(1-z)$:}

Near $z = 0$: $\csc^2 z = 1/\sin^2 z$. Since $\sin z = z - z^3/6 + \cdots$, we have $\sin^2 z = z^2 - z^4/3 + \cdots$, so $\csc^2 z$ has a pole of order 2 at $z=0$.

Also $\log(1-z) = -z - z^2/2 - z^3/3 - \cdots$, which has a zero of order 1 at $z = 0$.

The product $\csc^2 z \cdot \log(1-z)$ has a pole of order $2 - 1 = 1$ (\textbf{simple pole}).

To find the residue, expand:
\[
\frac{\log(1-z)}{\sin^2 z} = \frac{-z - z^2/2 - z^3/3 - \cdots}{z^2(1-z^2/3+\cdots)^2} \cdot \frac{1}{(1-z^2/3+\cdots)^{-2}}
\]

More carefully:
\[
\frac{1}{\sin^2 z} = \frac{1}{z^2}\cdot\frac{1}{(1-z^2/6+\cdots)^2} = \frac{1}{z^2}\left(1 + \frac{z^2}{3}+\cdots\right).
\]

\[
\frac{\log(1-z)}{\sin^2 z} = \frac{1}{z^2}\left(1+\frac{z^2}{3}+\cdots\right)\left(-z-\frac{z^2}{2}-\frac{z^3}{3}-\cdots\right)
\]
\[
= \frac{1}{z^2}\left(-z - \frac{z^2}{2} - \frac{z^3}{3} - \frac{z^3}{3} + \cdots\right) = -\frac{1}{z} - \frac{1}{2} - \frac{2z}{3} + \cdots
\]

Residue $= -1$.

Integral: $\oint_C \csc^2 z\log(1-z)\,dz = 2\pi i(-1) = \boxed{-2\pi i}$.

\textbf{(c) $\displaystyle\frac{\sin z - \tan z}{z}$:}

Expand near $z = 0$:
\[
\sin z = z - \frac{z^3}{6} + \frac{z^5}{120} - \cdots
\]
\[
\tan z = z + \frac{z^3}{3} + \frac{2z^5}{15} + \cdots
\]
\[
\sin z - \tan z = -\frac{z^3}{2} - \frac{z^5}{8} - \cdots
\]
\[
\frac{\sin z - \tan z}{z} = -\frac{z^2}{2} - \frac{z^4}{8} - \cdots
\]

This is analytic at $z = 0$ (no pole!). The function has a \textbf{removable singularity} at the origin.

Order of pole: none (order 0). Residue $= 0$.

Integral: $\oint_C \frac{\sin z - \tan z}{z}\,dz = \boxed{0}$.

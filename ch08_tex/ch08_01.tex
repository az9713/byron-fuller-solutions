\textbf{Chapter 8, Problem 1.} Prove that the space $C$ of continuous functions on $[a,b]$ with norm given by
\[
\|f\| = \max_{a\le x\le b} |f(x)|
\]
for $f\in C$ is a Banach space, i.e., show that it is complete with respect to the above norm.

\bigskip
\textbf{Solution.}

We must show that every Cauchy sequence $\{f_n\}$ in $C[a,b]$ converges to a limit in $C[a,b]$ under the sup norm $\|f\|=\max_{x\in[a,b]}|f(x)|$.

\textbf{Step 1: Pointwise convergence.}
Let $\{f_n\}$ be a Cauchy sequence in $C[a,b]$. Then for every $\varepsilon>0$, there exists $N$ such that for all $m,n>N$,
\[
\|f_m - f_n\| = \max_{x\in[a,b]}|f_m(x)-f_n(x)| < \varepsilon.
\]
In particular, for each fixed $x\in[a,b]$, $|f_m(x)-f_n(x)|\le\|f_m-f_n\|<\varepsilon$. Thus $\{f_n(x)\}$ is a Cauchy sequence of real (or complex) numbers and therefore converges. Define
\[
f(x) = \lim_{n\to\infty} f_n(x).
\]

\textbf{Step 2: Uniform convergence.}
From the Cauchy condition, for all $m,n>N$ and all $x\in[a,b]$:
\[
|f_m(x)-f_n(x)|<\varepsilon.
\]
Taking $m\to\infty$ with $n>N$ fixed:
\[
|f(x)-f_n(x)|\le\varepsilon \quad \text{for all } x\in[a,b].
\]
Thus $\|f-f_n\|\le\varepsilon$ for all $n>N$, which means $f_n\to f$ uniformly.

\textbf{Step 3: Continuity of the limit.}
Since each $f_n$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $f_n\to f$ uniformly, the uniform limit theorem guarantees that $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$. Therefore $f\in C[a,b]$.

Since every Cauchy sequence in $C[a,b]$ converges to an element of $C[a,b]$, the space is complete, hence a Banach space. $\blacksquare$

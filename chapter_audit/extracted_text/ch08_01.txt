Chapter 8, Problem 1. Prove that the space C of continuous functions on [a, b] with
norm given by
∥f ∥ = max
a≤x≤b
|f (x)|
for f ∈ C is a Banach space, i.e., show that it is complete with respect to the above norm.
Solution.
We must show that every Cauchy sequence {fn} in C[a, b] converges to a limit in C[a, b]
under the sup norm ∥f ∥ = max x∈[a,b] |f (x)|.
Step 1: Pointwise convergence. Let {fn} be a Cauchy sequence in C[a, b]. Then for
every ε > 0, there exists N such that for all m, n > N ,
∥fm − fn∥ = max
x∈[a,b]
|fm(x) − fn(x)| < ε.
In particular, for each fixed x ∈ [a, b], |fm(x) − fn(x)| ≤ ∥ fm − fn∥ < ε . Thus {fn(x)} is a
Cauchy sequence of real (or complex) numbers and therefore converges. Define
f (x) = lim
n→∞
fn(x).
Step 2: Uniform convergence. From the Cauchy condition, for all m, n > N and all
x ∈ [a, b]:
|fm(x) − fn(x)| < ε.
Taking m → ∞ with n > N fixed:
|f (x) − fn(x)| ≤ ε for all x ∈ [a, b].
Thus ∥f − fn∥ ≤ ε for all n > N , which means fn → f uniformly.
Step 3: Continuity of the limit. Since each fn is continuous on [a, b] and fn → f
uniformly, the uniform limit theorem guarantees that f is continuous on [a, b]. Therefore
f ∈ C[a, b].
Since every Cauchy sequence in C[a, b] converges to an element of C[a, b], the space is
complete, hence a Banach space. ■

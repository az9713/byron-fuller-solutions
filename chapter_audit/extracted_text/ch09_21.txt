Difference when q1 is real vs. complex: When q1 is real (physical scattering), the
kernel k(x, t) is real, so k(x1, x2)k(x2, x1) = |k(x1, x2)|2 ≥ 0, and ˜d2 < 0, meaning ˜D(λ) < 1
for small λ. The norm integral converges because of the exponential damping e−µr.
If q1 were complex (as in bound-state problems), the kernel would be complex, the norm
integral might not converge as readily, and the phase structure of ˜D(λ) would be more
intricate. The key difference is that for real q1, the kernel is manifestly real and the Hilbert–
Schmidt norm is straightforward to evaluate, while for complex q1 additional care with
convergence is needed. ■

Chapter 9, Problem 26
Byron & Fuller, Mathematics of Classical and Quantum Physics
Problem. Suppose that {ϕn} and {ψm} are sequences of step functions and that ϕn → f
and ψm → g. Show that for any step functions ϕ and ψ:
(V ϕ, ψ) = ⟨ϕ, U ψ⟩, (V ϕ, V ψ) = ⟨ϕ, ψ⟩, (U ϕ, U ψ) = ⟨ϕ, ψ⟩.
Then show that {V ϕn} and {U ψm} converge to limits V f and U g, and (V f, g) = ⟨f, U g⟩.
This completes the proof of Theorem 9.11.
Solution.
This problem concerns extending the Fourier transform operators U and V from step
functions to all of L2, thereby completing the proof that the Fourier transform defines a
unitary transformation on Hilbert space (Theorem 9.11).
Step 1: Properties on step functions
For step functions, U and V are defined by the Fourier transform and its inverse. The three
identities are consequences of the Parseval–Plancherel theorem for step functions:
(i) (V ϕ, V ψ) = ⟨ϕ, ψ⟩:
For step functions ϕ, ψ, V ϕ and V ψ are their Fourier transforms (or inverse transforms).

Step 2: Extension to L2
Since ϕn → f in L2, and V is an isometry on step functions ( ∥V ϕ∥ = ∥ϕ∥):
∥V ϕn − V ϕm∥ = ∥ϕn − ϕm∥ → 0 as n, m → ∞.
So {V ϕn} is a Cauchy sequence in L2. Since L2 is complete, V ϕn → V f for some V f ∈ L2.
This defines V f for all f ∈ L2.
Similarly, U ψm → U g for some U g ∈ L2.

Step 3: The identity (V f, g) = ⟨f, U g⟩
For step functions, (V ϕn, ψm) = ⟨ϕn, U ψm⟩. Taking limits:
• Left side: V ϕn → V f and ψm → g in L2, so (V ϕn, ψm) → (V f, g) (inner product is
continuous).
• Right side: ϕn → f and U ψm → U g, so ⟨ϕn, U ψm⟩ → ⟨ f, U g⟩.
Therefore:
(V f, g) = ⟨f, U g⟩ for all f, g ∈ L2 .
Similarly, from (V ϕn, V ϕm) = ⟨ϕn, ϕm⟩, taking limits gives (V f, V g) = ⟨f, g⟩, proving V
is an isometry on all of L2. The same holds for U . Since V † = U (from (V f, g) = ( f, U g))
and both are isometries, U and V are unitary transformations. This completes the proof of
Theorem 9.11. ■
